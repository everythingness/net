We’re at peak angst. Don’t panic, says Dr Gwendoline Smith

It’s clear as soon as you open Dr Gwendoline Smith’s The Book of Knowing that this clinical psychologist does not believe in pussyfooting around her clients. “If you don’t accept reality for what it is, you’re f***ed,” she writes. “Because the universe doesn’t care. It’s not just, nor is it fair. Otherwise, bad things would not happen to good people.”

Smith’s straight-talking approach and flamboyant appearance (think Prue Leith-style glasses and funky jewellery) have made her something of a household name in New Zealand where The Book of Knowing and its sequel, The Book of Overthinking, topped the bestseller lists (they also sold well in Britain). The last of the trilogy, The Book of Angst, is just out and expected to be another hit.

People listen to Smith, 66, a renowned expert in depression and anxiety, not least because she has direct experience of what she’s talking about. She is bipolar, meaning she suffers from violent mood swings. “Although I prefer to call it manic depression,” she says from her home in Auckland.

Smith suspects this manic depression was always there. “But I’m an extrovert and if you’re an extrovert it tends to get missed, because you’re just perceived as an eccentric. It was always, like, ‘Gwen’s a maverick.’ I’d just be rushing here, rushing there.”

Her first psychotic episode came when she was in her late thirties, when she suddenly became “very delusional. I’m an atheist but the night it started I was at a girlfriend’s and I sat down at 3am and said I was going to rewrite the Bible. I thought I was re-enacting the crucifixion on her fireplace and that I was the female reincarnation of Jesus. I would only talk to people named Peter or Paul or John and I wouldn’t have anything black in the house, I could only touch white things.”

She simultaneously concluded she was the product of a torrid affair between Elizabeth Taylor and Walt Disney, but was confused as she was the second Messiah, who must have been immaculately conceived. “I was mad,” she says perkily.

As she came down from this “high”, she became severely depressed. “I couldn’t eat, I couldn’t get out of bed. That’s when I knew this was totally an illness.”

Nonetheless, when a psychiatrist suggest medication, Smith ignored him. “Like many creative people, I hated the idea of anything interfering with my creativity.” The second time she experienced an “episode”, she was committed for a month to a psychiatric unit. “I wish I’d been in an asylum — they were solidly built and surrounded by open spaces and beautiful gardens. What I was in was a disgusting, inhumane building — the furniture was all ripped and this was built in the mid-Nineties.”

Between the two “lock-ups”, she was diagnosed with breast cancer, resulting in a double mastectomy. “But for me, the depression was worse. With breast cancer I still had a sense of humour and a concept of self, whereas with depression I would stay in bed for weeks, ignoring the world to the point of ignoring my appointment with the breast cancer clinic. It’s profoundly debilitating.”

In 2013 she was committed for a third time, for about a month. By then, with two marriages behind her, she was with her present partner, who is a composer (she has no children). “My behaviour really did give him a fright,” she says. This time, she agreed to take the mood-stabilising drug lithium. “And it really made a difference. In fact I’m disappointed I wasn’t put on it earlier.”

As a result of her experiences, Smith believes sometimes nothing but drugs can help with mental illness. “So many people don’t want to use medication, it’s a battle. But these drugs can be incredibly effective.”

Nonetheless, Smith has treated hundreds of patients — several from the UK, who Zoomed and Skyped her long before the pandemic — with cognitive behavioural therapy, a “talking therapy” that helps people to manage problems by changing the way they think and behave. The Book of Angst, which delineates the various types of anxiety, including obsessive-compulsive disorder, eating disorders, social anxiety and health anxiety formerly known as hypochondria, gives detailed, step-by-step instructions on how to follow such a programme. “Not everybody can afford therapy, but the way I’ve laid it out makes them feel I’m in the room with them.”

Publication couldn’t be more timely. Although the lack of hard data makes it impossible to quantify exactly how mental health has suffered, recent research by the University of Queensland in Australia estimates that while normally cases of anxiety and depression remain stable from year to year, in 2020 they rose by an average of 27 per cent globally. “I wrote the book during our first lockdown where everywhere you looked the world was just full of anxiety. People fearful: were they going to get Covid, was their business going to go under, were their kids going to get it?” Smith says. “Clinicians here have shut their books, they just can’t keep up with the demand.”

Yet Smith refuses to attribute this vast rise in anxiety to lockdowns alone. “Of course, many anxiety conditions have been exceptionally aggravated by Covid: people with obsessive-compulsive disorder were already washing their hands till they bleed, and then the government message is ‘Wash your hands, wash your hands’. Health anxiety has been turned on its head, everyone’s now vigilant about sneezing and sniffling and coughing. But it works both ways. In New Zealand we’ve been under lockdown, apart from essential services, for about four months, but some people who have social anxiety are having a ball, because they don’t have to see anybody.”

There’s a bracing bluntness to Smith, who was born in Chatham, Kent, where her father was in the navy. When she was eight her family became one of the 1.5 million “Ten Pound Poms” who emigrated down under, in search of a better life.

She has since returned to the UK, to do research at Oxford University, and wondered if she might resettle here. “But I saw I might be at home in a university setting but not hanging out with my relatives who eat really nasty food from Tesco’s,” she says.

Have her mental-health experiences made her a better psychologist? “Because I have the combination of both sides, it is a bit of a gift but as a clinician I don’t talk about myself — that’s not what we do. I’ll say, ‘This is your therapy session, not mine.’ It’s only sometimes if the client needs reassurance, if perhaps they’re concerned they’re getting more manic, that I might use my experience either to say, ‘It’s time to visit a psychiatrist’ or, ‘It sounds like you’re just excited about your new job and need to keep an eye on your sleep.’ ”

Since Smith started practising in 1981, many new bogeymen have entered the room. “My practice is filled up with kids and I’d say around 70 per cent of them suffer from social anxiety and that’s largely been brought on by social media,” she says.

“Nowadays you can be judged everywhere. There’s a lot of thinking, ‘Oh look at them, they’ve got all these friends and all these likes, everything’s fabulous.’ I really like that Roosevelt quote, ‘Comparison is the thief of joy.’ The Facebook whistleblower [Frances Haugen, who recently revealed how social media’s algorithms worked to pull people — particularly children — down psychological rabbit holes] has really exposed an agenda that Facebook has always denied. And I don’t think there’s ever been a source of bullying as evil as cyber-bullying. Once you got a couple of boys in the schoolyard, one ends up with a broken nose and there’s a bit of a kerfuffle, and girls have always had that tendency to use exclusion and bitchiness, but before it might have been in a group of four. Now, one push and the whole school knows about it. So you do wonder if when life was simpler when you avoided a lot of these conditions.”

Yet, Smith adds, we can’t blame Mark Zuckerberg for our woes. “Let’s face it, there’s a lot of kids mucking around on social media that really just don’t give a shit.” After all, for many of us, there’s a big genetic predisposition at play. In fact, she says, psychologists estimate between 25 per cent and 40 per cent of all anxiety disorders are genetic in origin, while in obsessive-compulsive disorder the genetic predisposition is estimated to be as high as between 65 and 80 per cent. “It’s not to say if a relative suffers, you will suffer, but your risk is heightened.”

It’s for that reason that Smith has campaigned in New Zealand to destigmatise mental illness, infuriated by the rubbernecking in the chemist when she went to collect some pills. “It really pisses me off that mental health conditions have genetic contributions that can be higher than 40 per cent. That figure’s higher than in cardiology, than in respiratory. Yet mental health is still the ugly duckling of medicine.”

But that’s changing, to the point where she wonders if soaring anxiety statistics may simply mean we’ve become better at diagnosis. “Most kids today think nothing of talking to a therapist. I think both my parents had complex post-traumatic stress disorders, they’d spent their childhoods getting bombed by the Germans. But nobody talked about it. Everybody was just getting pissed.”

Just listening to Smith makes me feel much better. How does she avoid anxiety? “I thrive on laughing and having fun and I place a great deal of value on my friendships. I make a contribution. I love helping people, hence I love my work.”
The Book of Angst by Gwendoline Smith is published by Atlantic Books at £8.99
